People have been portraying themselves and others for as long as art has existed













At the turn of the 20th century, photography had become the most accessible and popular medium for portraiture

Photography (from the Greek words phos, meaning “light”, and graphê meaning “drawing or writing”) which literally translates to "drawing with ligth", allowed for a more realistic and accurate depiction of what we see.

As though photography freed artists from the burden of producing realistic depictions, many late-19th- and early-20th-century artists began to experiment with new ways to portray people on canvas.

Many artists sought to represent the character and psychology of their sitters; similarly, when portraying themselves, they aimed at communicating something of their innermost selves.

The attention for subjectivity and emotions paired with the eagerness to break with the tradition, typical of the late 19th century and the avant-garde movements, created the conditions for a radical transformation in the portraiture genre in modern art.

COLOURS

When it comes to painting, the colours used in a work of art are just as important as the subject matter.

Although colour has always been an important part of the art-making process, the variety of tones that are now readily accessible were not available in the past.

The varieties of pigments available to artists before the 20th century were limited because the majority of colors were created using pricey and challenging-to-process natural ingredients.

The invention of synthetic pigments and paint tubes during the Industrial Revolution allowed the Impressionists and succeeding generations of artists to paint outside of their studios and offered a greater range of colors at more competitive prices.

The abandonment of artistic convention in favor of experimentation that happened across movements during the 20th century was somewhat influenced by this colour revolution.

Throughout history, certain colors have maintained symbolic associations that are often reflected in art. Many artists have incorporated colour into their artwork to elicit certain emotions.

For instance, Picasso's use of only blue hues to show depressing and disheartening themes from 1901 to 1904 and the use of red, pink, and orange hues to depict a happier and cheerful mood from 1904 to 1906 are good examples of how color affects emotion.

Colours may evoke emotions that words cannot quite match. Depending on the viewer and where they are from, each hue has a positive and negative significance that can trigger a variety of reactions and emotions.

  • RED - usually symbolizes power, passion, love, adventure, and energy; but can also symbolize anger, seduction, violence, and danger.
  • YELLOW - is typically is a symbol of happiness, enlightenment, joy, positivity, and clarity. It can also symbolize being critical, judgmental, or cautious.
  • ORANGE - is typically is a symbol of enthusiasm, fascination, creativity, determination, success, and stimulation. Its negative meaning can be abrasive and crass.
  • GREEN - is a symbol of life, renewal, nature, growth, balance and harmony. It can also be a symbol of greed, jealousy, and possessiveness.
  • BLUE - is typically a symbol of depth, stability, wisdom, faith, truth, and heaven. The negative side can be coldness, uncaring, and sadness.
  • PURPLE - it combines the stability of blue and the fierce energy of red, creating royalty, nobility, pride, luxury, and ambition. The negative side is decadence, conceit, and pomposity.
