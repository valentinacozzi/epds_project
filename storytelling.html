People have been portraying themselves and others for as long as art has existed

At the turn of the 20th century, photography had become the most accessible and popular medium for portraiture

Photography (from the Greek words phos, meaning “light”, and graphê meaning “drawing or writing”) which literally translates to "drawing with ligth", allowed for a more realistic and accurate depiction of what we see.

As though photography freed artists from the burden of producing realistic depictions, many late-19th- and early-20th-century artists began to experiment with new ways to portray people on canvas.

Many artists sought to represent the character and psychology of their sitters; similarly, when portraying themselves, they aimed at communicating something of their innermost selves.

The attention for subjectivity and emotions paired with the eagerness to break with the tradition, typical of the late 19th century and the avant-garde movements, created the conditions for a radical transformation in the portraiture genre in modern art.
